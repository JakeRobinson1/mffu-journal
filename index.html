01 — Strategy Guide
What Is The ORB Strategy?

ORB stands for Opening Range Breakout. The idea is simple — when the US stock market opens at 14:30 UK time, the first 15 minutes of trading forms a range between a high and a low price. This is your Opening Range (OR).

When price breaks convincingly above or below that range and then comes back to retest the level, you enter a trade in the direction of the breakout. You risk 10 points to make 20 points — a 1:2 risk to reward ratio.

The instrument is MES — the Micro E-mini S&P 500 futures contract. Each point is worth $5 per contract.

Step 1 — Pre-Session Preparation (Before 14:30)
1
Check the economic calendar
Go to forexfactory.com and check today's date. If there is a red (high impact) USD news event — especially NFP, CPI, or FOMC — do not trade today. Log it as a skip.
2
Mark overnight levels on your chart
On your 15-minute chart, mark the overnight high (highest point from yesterday's 16:00 close to today's 14:30 open) and the overnight low (lowest point in that same period). Also note the previous day's closing price.
3
Assess the overnight trend
Look left on your chart. Has price been trending up or down all morning? A clear directional trend gives you a bias for which direction to trade. No clear trend means the session is harder to trade.
Step 2 — Mark The Opening Range (At 14:30)
4
Wait for the 14:30 candle to close
On a 15-minute chart, the candle that opens at 14:30 closes at 14:45. This single candle defines your Opening Range. Mark a horizontal line at its HIGH and another at its LOW. These are your OR high and OR low.
5
Note double-confirmed levels
If the OR high is at the same price as the overnight high — that is a double-confirmed level. These setups have higher conviction and are your best A+ opportunities.
Step 3 — Wait For A Valid Entry (14:45 to 15:30)
6
Wait for a clean breakout close
Price must close clearly above the OR high (for a long trade) or below the OR low (for a short trade) on your 5-minute chart. A wick through the level does not count — the candle body must close beyond the level.
7
Wait for the retest
After the breakout, price will often pull back to retest the OR level it just broke. This retest is where you enter. No retest means no trade — do not chase the breakout.
8
Confirm the retest candle
The retest candle must close in the direction of your trade. For a long — the candle must close above the OR high. For a short — the candle must close below the OR low. A large candle body closing well away from the level means sellers/buyers won that candle — skip it.
9
Enter the trade
Enter at the OR level on the retest. Set your stop loss 10 points below your entry (for a long) or 10 points above (for a short). Set your take profit 20 points in the direction of your trade.
Step 4 — Manage The Trade
10
Move stop to breakeven at +10 points
Once price moves 10 points in your favour (your 1:1 level), move your stop loss to your entry price plus 1-2 points to cover fees. This makes the trade risk-free — the worst outcome is now a tiny profit.
11
Let the ATM run to take profit
Do not close the trade early. Do not move your take profit. Let the trade run to your 20 point target. The hardest part of trading is doing nothing — trust the process.
12
One trade per day maximum
If your first trade loses, close the platform and walk away. Do not try to recover losses the same day. Come back tomorrow with a fresh mindset.
02 — Glossary
Key Terms Explained
${[
OR / Opening Range
The high and low price of the 14:30 candle on a 15-minute chart. Defines the boundaries you are trading the breakout of.
OR High
The highest price reached during the 14:30 candle. A long trade triggers when price breaks above this level.
OR Low
The lowest price reached during the 14:30 candle. A short trade triggers when price breaks below this level.
Overnight High
The highest price reached between yesterday's 16:00 close and today's 14:30 open.
Overnight Low
The lowest price reached between yesterday's 16:00 close and today's 14:30 open.
Previous Day Close (PDC)
The price at which the market closed yesterday at 16:00. Acts as a magnet — price tends to gravitate toward it.
Breakout
When price moves clearly beyond the OR high or OR low with a candle body closing outside the range.
Retest
After a breakout, price pulls back to the level it just broke. This pullback is called the retest and is where you enter.
Confirmation Candle
The candle that forms during the retest. It must close in the direction of your trade to confirm the entry is valid.
Long
A trade where you buy, expecting price to go up.
Short
A trade where you sell, expecting price to go down.
Stop Loss (SL)
A price level where your trade automatically closes to limit your loss. Set 10 points from your entry.
Take Profit (TP)
A price level where your trade automatically closes to lock in profit. Set 20 points from your entry.
Breakeven (BE)
Moving your stop loss to your entry price once the trade is +10 points. Eliminates risk on the trade.
R:R (Risk to Reward)
The ratio of your potential loss to potential gain. This strategy uses 1:2 — risk 1 to make 2.
MES
Micro E-mini S&P 500. A futures contract worth $5 per point. Easier to start with than the full-size ES contract.
ES

ATM Strategy
Automated Trade Management — a NinjaTrader feature that automatically sets your stop loss and take profit when you enter a trade.
Setup Grade
A rating (A+, A, B, C, F) that describes the quality of a potential trade setup. Only take A and A+ grades.
Skip
A session where you choose not to trade because the conditions are not right. Skips protect your capital.
NFP / CPI / FOMC
High impact US economic news events that cause unpredictable price movements. Never trade on these days.
Drawdown
The maximum amount your account can lose before it is closed by the prop firm. Protect it at all times.
03 — Setup Examples
A+ Grade Setup — Highest Conviction

This is the setup you wait for. All conditions are perfectly aligned.

✓ Strong overnight trend in one clear direction
✓ OR high/low coincides with overnight high/low (double-confirmed level)
✓ Clean, decisive breakout candle closing clearly beyond the level
✓ Clean retest that holds the level
✓ Confirmation candle closes convincingly in trade direction
✓ Previous day close is in the direction of travel (acts as magnet)
Take this trade every time it appears. These run cleanly to target.
A Grade Setup — Strong

A solid setup with most conditions aligned. Worth taking.

✓ Clear overnight trend in one direction
✓ Clean breakout close beyond OR level
✓ Retest holds the level cleanly
✓ Confirmation candle closes in trade direction
~ OR level may not coincide with overnight level
Take this trade. Not as high conviction as A+ but still a valid setup with edge.
B Grade Setup — Marginal

Some conditions present but one or more key elements are missing or weak.

~ Overnight trend present but not strong or clear
~ Breakout candle present but not fully convincing
~ Retest present but candle is large or slightly away from level
✗ May be counter trend against the overnight move
Skip this trade. Backtest data shows B grade setups have a poor win rate. The risk is not worth it.
C Grade Setup — Poor

Multiple conditions missing. Not a tradeable setup.

✗ Choppy overnight price action with no clear direction
✗ Price consolidating within the OR — no breakout
✗ No clean retest — price just drifts through the level
✗ Counter trend trade with no confluence
Skip immediately. No setup means no trade.
F Grade — Automatic Skip

These sessions must never be traded. The risk is completely unmanageable.

✗ Explosive news-driven candle at the open (20+ points in one candle)
✗ NFP, CPI, or FOMC day
✗ High impact news event firing within your session window
✗ Massive wicks appearing randomly throughout the session
Close the platform immediately. These sessions destroy accounts.
04 — Common Mistakes
Mistakes Every New Trader Makes
Entering on a wick, not a candle close
A wick through the OR level means price briefly touched it but rejected. Only a candle body CLOSING beyond the level is a valid breakout. Entering on wicks leads to being stopped out constantly.
Chasing the breakout without a retest
When price breaks out strongly and runs away it feels like you're missing out. Resist the urge to chase it. The retest is your entry point — without it you are entering at the worst possible price with no edge.
Moving the stop loss wider to avoid being stopped out
When price approaches your stop loss it feels painful. Moving it wider feels like giving the trade more room. In reality you are increasing your loss and breaking your risk management rules. Never widen a stop loss.
Closing early for a small profit
When a trade is +5 points it feels tempting to close it and "lock in profit." But your strategy requires 20 points to maintain the 1:2 R:R that makes it profitable. Closing at +5 destroys your edge over time.
Trading news days
NFP, CPI, and FOMC days create violent unpredictable price spikes. No strategy works reliably on these days. Check Forex Factory every single day before your session. If you see red USD events — do not trade.
Revenge trading after a loss
After a losing trade the urge to immediately make it back is powerful and dangerous. This leads to forcing setups that aren't there and taking bigger risks. The rule is one trade per day — a loss means the session is over.
Trading every day regardless of conditions
Most days will not have a valid setup. Skipping is not failure — it is discipline. The traders who make consistent money are not the ones who trade the most. They are the ones who trade only when conditions are right.
Moving the breakeven stop too early
Moving your stop to breakeven before price reaches +10 points means normal trade fluctuation will stop you out for zero profit on trades that would have hit the full target. Only move to breakeven at exactly the 1:1 level.
Watching too much trading content online
YouTube and social media are full of traders showing different strategies, indicators, and techniques. Consuming too much of this creates analysis paralysis and makes you doubt your own strategy constantly. Pick one approach, learn it deeply, and ignore everything else.
05 — Psychology
The Truth About Trading Psychology

Most people who start trading assume the hard part is finding a good strategy. It isn't. The hard part is executing that strategy consistently when real money is on the line and your emotions are fighting against every rule you set.

A strategy that wins 60% of the time will still lose 40% of the time. Your brain will interpret those losses as evidence the strategy doesn't work and push you to change it, override it, or abandon it entirely. This is how profitable strategies get destroyed — not by the market, but by the trader.

The traders who make consistent money are not smarter than you. They have simply learned to manage their emotions so that their behaviour stays consistent regardless of what the market does.

The Emotions You Will Feel — And How To Handle Them
😤 FOMO — Fear Of Missing Out
What it feels like: Price breaks out strongly and runs 15 points without you. You feel like you missed a perfect trade and consider chasing it.
The reality: Without a retest you have no edge. You would be buying at the top of a move with your stop in the worst possible place.
Remind yourself: no retest, no trade. The next setup is coming.
😰 Fear — When A Trade Goes Against You
What it feels like: You enter a trade and it immediately moves against you. Your unrealised loss grows and you want to close the trade to stop the pain.
The reality: All trades experience heat — price moving against you before it moves in your direction. Your stop loss is there for a reason. A valid setup does not become invalid because price dips 3 points against you.
Remind yourself: if the stop hasn't been hit the trade is still valid. Do nothing.
😡 Anger — After A Losing Trade
What it feels like: You followed every rule and the trade still lost. You feel frustrated, cheated, and tempted to immediately enter another trade to win it back.
The reality: Losses are a normal part of trading. A strategy with a 60% win rate loses 40% of the time — that means roughly 4 out of every 10 trades will lose. The loss is not a signal that the strategy is broken. It is the cost of doing business.
Remind yourself: one loss means the session is over. Close the platform and come back tomorrow.
🤩 Overconfidence — After A Winning Streak
What it feels like: You have won 5 trades in a row. You feel invincible. You start taking B and C grade setups because you are on a roll. You increase your position size beyond your rules.
The reality: Winning streaks end. The market does not care about your recent performance. Deviating from your rules during a hot streak is one of the most common ways traders blow accounts — they give back months of gains in a few reckless days.
Remind yourself: follow the rules on trade 50 exactly the same as trade 1. The process never changes.
😔 Boredom — On Skip Days
What it feels like: You sit at your desk for an hour and the setup never appears. You feel like you wasted your time. You consider entering a marginal setup just to feel like you did something.
The reality: Approximately 70% of trading sessions will not have a valid setup. Sitting through those sessions without trading is not wasted time — it is active capital preservation. Every skip protects your account for when the A+ setup appears.
Remind yourself: a skip is not a missed opportunity. It is a protected opportunity.
😟 Self Doubt — When The Strategy Goes Through A Slow Patch
What it feels like: Two or three weeks pass with very few valid setups or a couple of losses. You start to think the strategy doesn't work, that you are not cut out for trading, or that you should try something else.
The reality: All strategies go through slow periods. January 2026 was one of the most difficult months in recent memory for this strategy — news events, volatile price action, very few clean setups. Traders who survived that month and kept following their rules came out the other side into cleaner conditions.
Remind yourself: look at your backtest data. The edge is proven. Trust the data, not the feeling.
Building A Healthy Trading Mindset
→
Focus on process, not outcome
A good trade is one where you followed all your rules correctly — regardless of whether it won or lost. A bad trade is one where you broke your rules — regardless of whether it won or lost. Judge yourself on process, not profit.
→
Think in probabilities, not certainties
No trade is guaranteed to win. Even the best A+ setup can lose. What matters is that over 100 trades your winners outweigh your losers. Each individual trade is just one data point in a much larger sample.
→
Never risk money you cannot afford to lose
If the money in your trading account represents rent, bills, or essential living costs your emotions will be too heightened to trade correctly. Only trade with money whose loss would not affect your life. This is why starting with a prop firm evaluation makes sense — your personal financial risk is capped at the evaluation fee.
→
Keep a journal — this one
Logging every trade forces you to be honest with yourself about your decisions. When you know you have to write down why you entered, you make better decisions. Patterns in your journal — certain emotions before losing trades, certain conditions when you break rules — become visible over time and allow you to improve.
→
Detach from the money
Think in terms of R (risk units) rather than dollars. A win is always +2R. A loss is always -1R. Whether 1R equals $50 or $500 is irrelevant to the quality of your decision. Traders who think in dollars make emotional decisions. Traders who think in R make logical ones.
→
Accept that patience is the strategy
The biggest competitive advantage you have over most traders is the willingness to do nothing. Most retail traders overtrade, take marginal setups, and let emotions drive their decisions. Simply waiting for A and A+ grade setups and executing them correctly puts you ahead of the majority. Patience is not a personality trait — it is a skill you build through backtesting and screen time.
The One Thing To Remember

"The strategy is not the edge.
Consistent execution of the strategy is the edge."

Anyone can find a profitable strategy. Very few people can execute it consistently under real money pressure for months and years. That consistency is what trading psychology is about — and it is built one disciplined decision at a time.
